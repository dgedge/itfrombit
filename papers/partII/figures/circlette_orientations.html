Oriented Circlettes: How Rings Fit Together

Each circlette is an 8-bit ring with a definite reading direction and specific bit positions around the circumference. When particles bind, the parity checks connect specific bit positions on neighbouring rings. This means the rings must be oriented so that the interacting bit sectors face each other — like puzzle pieces that only fit one way.

1. The Ring Layout

The 8 bits are arranged on the ring in a specific order. The bit positions define sectors around the circumference:

The circlette ring with its 8 bit positions. The ring has three sectors: Generation (brown, top), Colour (red, bottom-right), and Electroweak (blue, left), connected by the Bridge bit (green, right). Physical interactions couple through specific sectors.

Key insight: Each type of force couples through a specific sector of the ring. The strong force connects through the colour sector (C₀, C₁). The weak force acts through the bridge-isospin boundary (LQ, I₃). Electromagnetic interactions couple through charge-carrying bits (LQ, I₃). This means rings must orient with the relevant sector facing the interaction partner.

2. The Proton: Three Rings with Colour Sectors Facing Inward

In a proton (uud), three quark circlettes are bound by the strong force through their colour bits. The parity check enforcing colour neutrality (r⊕g⊕b = white) connects the C₀,C₁ bits of each quark. For this check to be local on the lattice, the colour sectors of all three rings must face the centre of the triangle.

The proton as three oriented circlettes. Each ring is rotated so its colour sector (C₀, C₁ — highlighted) faces the centre, where the colour parity check r⊕g⊕b = 00 is enforced. The generation sectors face outward; the electroweak sectors face tangentially. The rings are not free to rotate — the strong force locks their orientation.

The strong force locks ring orientation. In a baryon, each quark ring must be rotated so its colour sector faces the binding centre. This is not a choice — it's a requirement for the colour parity check to be local. The three rings in a proton have three different orientations, 120° apart, creating a triangular tiling with three-fold symmetry.

3. The Consequences of Orientation Locking

3.1 What faces outward?

If the colour sectors face inward (toward the binding centre), then the generation and electroweak sectors face outward — toward the rest of the universe. This is physically correct and significant:

The electroweak sector (I₃, χ, W) faces outward, meaning the weak interaction and electromagnetic charge are the properties visible to external particles. The proton's charge (+1), its weak interactions, and its ability to participate in beta decay are all mediated through the outward-facing electroweak bits.

The generation sector (G₀, G₁) also faces outward. Generation is what distinguishes the electron from the muon from the tau — it sets the mass scale. The outward-facing generation bits determine how the composite interacts with the Higgs mechanism (chirality mixing) and hence what mass it presents to the world.

The colour sector is hidden in the interior. External particles cannot directly access the colour bits — they're pointing inward, locked in mutual parity checks with the other quarks. This is colour confinement as geometry: you can't see the colour because the colour bits literally face away from you, into the binding region.

Colour confinement is geometric. The colour bits face inward, locked in mutual parity checks. External particles interact only with the outward-facing electroweak and generation sectors. The "invisibility" of colour charge to external probes is not a mysterious dynamical screening effect — it is the simple geometric fact that the relevant bits point into the interior of the baryon.

3.2 Electron bonding to the proton

When an electron circlette binds to the proton (forming hydrogen), it couples through the electromagnetic interaction — which connects to the charge-carrying bits (LQ, I₃). The electron's LQ and I₃ bits face the proton. The proton's outward-facing electroweak sectors include I₃, which carries the charge information. So the electron-proton bond is mediated by the electroweak sectors of the proton's constituent quarks facing the electron's corresponding sector.

3.3 The CNOT gate orientation

The weak interaction (the CNOT gate I₃ → I₃ ⊕ LQ) acts on the bridge-isospin boundary — bits 3 and 6 on the ring, which are on opposite sides of the ring (the bridge is in the generation-colour boundary region; isospin is in the electroweak sector). In a baryon, the LQ bit faces inward (adjacent to the colour sector) while I₃ faces outward. The CNOT gate therefore operates across the ring, connecting the interior (colour-bound) region to the exterior (electroweak) region. This is why the weak force can convert quarks to leptons: it reaches across the ring from the outward-facing isospin to the inward-facing bridge, and flipping the bridge bit effectively unlocks the ring from its colour binding.

4. Covalent Bonds: Electroweak Sectors Facing Each Other

In a covalent bond (e.g., H₂), two electron circlettes share parity checks. The electron has no colour (C₀ = C₁ = 0, LQ = 0), so the colour sector is trivial. The bond is mediated through the electroweak sector — specifically through the charge-carrying bits and the chirality/weak bits that determine how the electron interacts with the lattice's syndrome field.

For two electrons to share parity checks (forming a bond), their electroweak sectors must face each other. This creates a specific geometric constraint on the relative orientation of the two electron rings.

A covalent bond between two electrons. The electroweak sectors (blue, highlighted) face each other across the bond region, enabling shared parity checks. The electrons must have opposite embedding orientations (spin ↑↓). Generation and colour sectors face outward.

5. The Orientation Rules

Summarising the geometric constraints:

Each force has a geometric signature. The strong force creates inward-facing colour triangles. Covalent bonds create facing electroweak pairs. The weak force reaches across the ring from interior to exterior. The geometry of the ring — which sectors are adjacent, which are opposite — determines which interactions are possible and how the rings must orient to participate in them.

This means molecular geometry is not imposed by spatial orbitals — it is a consequence of how oriented circlette rings can tile the lattice while satisfying all their parity-check constraints simultaneously.
