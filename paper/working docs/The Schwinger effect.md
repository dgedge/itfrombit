# The Schwinger effect

The **Schwinger effect** (or Schwinger mechanism) is a prediction of Quantum Electrodynamics (QED) that states a strong enough electric field can create matter from "nothing" (the vacuum).

This essentially predicts that extreme energy can "rip" particle-antiparticle pairs out of the vacuum.

### 1. What is the Schwinger Effect?

In QED, the vacuum isn't empty; it's a bubbling soup of "virtual" particle pairs (like an electron and a positron) that pop into existence and immediately annihilate each other.

- **The Mechanism:** If you apply an incredibly strong electric field (the "Schwinger Limit," approx $10^{18}$ volts/meter), the field pulls the positive positron one way and the negative electron the other way.
- **The "Rip":** If the field is strong enough, it pulls them apart so fast that they can't crash back together. The energy of the field is converted into the mass of the particles ($E=mc^2$), and they become "real" matter.

### 2. Recent Experimental "Detection"

The effect has been demonstrated in experiments that simulated the effect using **analogue systems**, because the field strength required for the *pure* Schwinger effect is still too high for current lasers. However, there are som special cases:

- **Graphene (2022/2023):** Researchers at the University of Manchester observed a Schwinger-like effect in **graphene**. Because electrons in graphene move effectively without mass (like light), the "threshold" to rip them out is much lower. They applied high currents and saw pairs of charge carriers (electrons and holes) appearing spontaneously.
- **Superfluid Helium (2024/2025):** Very recently, researchers have proposed and begun testing using **superfluid helium** as a vacuum analogue. In this setup, the "flow" of the fluid acts like the electric field, and "vortices" (little whirlpools) act like the particles. They observed these vortices popping into existence from the smooth flow, exactly as Schwinger predicted for particles.
